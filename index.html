Cervical Fluid

What it is and why it’s vital for your health and well‐being.

What is cervical fluid?

Cervical fluid, often called cervical mucus, is a healthy secretion produced by your cervix beginning at puberty. Regular cervical fluid self exams are an important way to keep tabs on your reproductive health and can even help you prevent pregnancy or get pregnant. Believe it or not, you already interact with this fluid every day when you wipe! But where does it come from?

Cervical fluid & your cycle

Have you ever noticed dried stains or liquid in your undewear? Or a slick feeling when you wipe? That’s cervical fluid!

It undergoes noticeable changes throughout your menstrual cycle—including changes in both its look and feel—in response to the female sex hormones estrogen and progesterone.

Every cervical fluid pattern is unique, and it will even be different from cycle to cycle among individuals. Next up, you’ll learn how and why your body produces this fluid.

The follicular phase

The first half of your menstrual cycle, or the follicular phase, is governed by the hormone estrogen. Several ovarian follicles—or tiny sacs in your ovaries that each house an immature egg—develop in the weeks before ovulation.

As your follicles develop, they produce estrogen. This triggers your cervix to produce cervical fluid, which may appear sticky or creamy at first. As ovulation gets closer, your follicles produce increasing amounts of estrogen, which causes your cervical fluid to become wet and slippery.

Ovulation

A few days before you ovulate, your cervical fluid will become wet and slippery. It may also be stretchy or thin and watery. You might even feel leaky and notice it in your underwear or when you wipe. This slippery cervical fluid is very hospitable to sperm and indicates that you are highly fertile.

During ovulation, your body releases a matured egg, or an ovum. An ovum will only live for 12 to 24 hours after ovulation. but slippery cervical fluid helps sperm reach it before it dies. Sperm can also live in cervical fluid for several days waiting for ovulation.

The luteal phase

The second half of your menstrual cycle, or the luteal phase, is governed mainly by the hormone progesterone. After you ovulate, the sac that released the ovum will take on a brand new role.

It becomes a structure called the corpus luteum, which produces the hormone progesterone. This hormone dries up your cervical fluid and thickens the lining of your uterus in case the ovum is fertilized.

Your cervical fluid may be sticky or creamy after ovulation, but it will no longer be wet and slippery.

Know your fluid.

Getting acquainted with your unique cervical fluid pattern is both easy to do and has some amazing health benefits.

 

Spot a problem.

Becoming familiar with your unique cervical fluid pattern can help you uncover reproductive issues or hormonal disorders that may require a visit to your doctor. Sudden or abnormal changes in your cervical fluid can help you identify signs of a vaginal infection, Pelvic Inflammatory Disease, hormone imbalance (like polycystic ovarian syndrome, or PCOS), and even potential infertility.

 

Get pregnant.

Fertile cervical fluid is vital for sperm survival and transport, so it plays a huge role in helping you get pregnant. Daily cervical fluid self exams help you pinpoint the most fertile days of your cycle, maximizing your chances of conceiving quickly. Familiarity with your fluid can even guide you and your doctor in the diagnosis and treatment of infertility.

 

Prevent pregnancy.

The ability to distinguish fertile cervical fluid can help you prevent pregnancy without the use of hormones. Fertility awareness‐based methods, such as the sympto‐thermal method (STM), rival hormonal contraceptives in effectiveness when practiced properly. Practicing STM helps you accurately pinpoint your fertile days with observations of cervical fluid and basal body temperature, and is backed by reproductive science. Learn more about STM.

 

Support your health.

Don’t let the ‘ick’ factor get to you. Like breast self exams, cervical fluid self exams provide important information about your health. Cervical fluid is nothing to be ashamed of. Talk to the the people in your life about its importance, and help us turn healthy cervical fluid into something that’s celebrated.
